Places

From an eternal flame in the desert to ruins of the Silk Road.

#1 Must Visit
1

Darvaza, Ahal Region

The Darvaza
Gas Crater

"The Door to Hell"

Perhaps the most jaw-dropping sight in all of Central Asia: a 230-foot-wide crater in the middle of the Karakum Desert that has been burning continuously since 1971, when Soviet geologists accidentally drilled into a natural gas pocket and set it alight to prevent methane spread.

The crater glows like a portal to another world — especially at night, when its flickering orange light illuminates the surrounding dunes for kilometers. Camping beside it under a star-blanketed sky is a once-in-a-lifetime experience.

Why It's #1: Nowhere else on Earth will you find a burning crater this vast and dramatic. It is simultaneously terrifying and beautiful — the defining symbol of wild, untamed Turkmenistan.
2

Capital City

Ashgabat —
The White City

"City of White Marble"

Turkmenistan's capital holds a Guinness World Record for having the most white marble-clad buildings in the world. The city feels like a sci-fi film set — gleaming towers, golden statues, enormous monuments, and bizarrely empty eight-lane boulevards lined with fountains.

Only white or pale-colored cars are permitted in the city. The Presidential Palace, Arch of Neutrality, and the National Museum are highlights. The Tolkuchka Bazaar on the outskirts is the chaotic, colorful antithesis of the marble city.

Why It's #2: No city on Earth looks quite like Ashgabat. Equal parts awe-inspiring and surreal, it is a living monument to power, identity, and the extremes of state architecture.

#3 Must Visit
3

Mary Region · UNESCO World Heritage

Ancient Merv —
Queen of the World

"The Pearl of the East"

Once among the largest cities on Earth and a legendary crossroads of the Great Silk Road, ancient Merv is a UNESCO World Heritage Site covering 60 km² of ruins spanning 4,000 years of civilization. It was successively ruled by Persians, Alexander the Great, Parthians, Arabs, and Seljuk Turks.

The ruins include massive city walls, the Sultan Sanjar Mausoleum, and layers of ancient cities built atop each other. At its medieval peak, Merv may have been the world's most populous city with over one million inhabitants.

Why It's #3: To walk through Merv is to walk through millennia of human history — empires, religions, and cultures all left their mark on this extraordinary oasis site.
4

Dashoguz Region · UNESCO World Heritage

Kunya-Urgench —
Towers of Eternity

"Old Urgench"

Another UNESCO World Heritage Site, Kunya-Urgench was the ancient capital of the Khorezm Empire and a major Silk Road hub from the 10th to 14th centuries. Devastated by Genghis Khan in 1221 and again by Timur (Tamerlane) in the 1380s.

The Kutlug-Timur Minaret — at 62 meters, the tallest in Central Asia — rises like a sentinel over the plains. The elaborate portal facades and tiled domes are some of the finest Islamic architecture to survive in the region.

Why It's #4: A deeply moving site — its solitary towers rising from the empty steppe carry the weight of an empire's fall. The architecture rivals anything in Samarkand or Bukhara.

#5 Must Visit
5

Canyons

Yangykala —
The Hidden Village

"Fiery Fortresses"

The unique attraction is located 165 km north of Balkanabat and 160 km east of Turkmenbashi and is popular with both local residents and tourists. The view of space landscapes, which you can admire to your heart's content, climbing a narrow ledge, protruding with a sharp edge above a high cliff, is breathtaking.

Yangi-Kala resembles a gigantic and impregnable fortress, erected by nature itself many centuries ago from layers of red, white, yellow and orange shades. The color of the rocks of the canyon is dominated by red, so the locals often call it Kyzildag (Red Mountains). In ancient times, the canyon was flooded with the waters of the Kara-Bogaz-Gol Bay, so even now especially lucky travelers manage to find here petrified shells and stones of mysterious shape and color. Many of those who were lucky enough to admire Yangi-Kala, note the echo of rare beauty, which repeats the enthusiastic exclamations of tourists many times.

Why It's #5: For its breathtaking “fire fortress” cliffs glowing in surreal red and pink hues.
