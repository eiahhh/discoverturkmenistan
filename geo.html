Geography

Turkmenistan is situated in Central Asia, bordered by Kazakhstan to the northwest, Uzbekistan to the north and east, Afghanistan to the southeast, Iran to the south, and the Caspian Sea to the west. Its geographic coordinates range from 35°08' to 42°48' north latitude and 52°27' to 66°41' east longitude. The country covers approximately 491,120 square kilometers (189,620 sq mi), making it the second largest Central Asian state after Kazakhstan.

The country is dominated by the Karakum Desert, which covers nearly 80% of its territory — one of the largest sand deserts in the world. Despite this arid landscape, Turkmenistan is rich in natural gas and oil.

The Kopet Dag mountain range forms its southern border with Iran, offering striking highland terrain. The Amu Darya River in the east provides fertile strips amid the desert, nurturing ancient oasis cities.

Languages

The Turkmen language is the official language — it belongs to the Oghuz branch of the Turkic family, closely related to Turkish and Azerbaijani. Turkmenistan uniquely uses a Latin-based script, adopted in 1993 after independence.

Russian remains widely spoken in urban areas — a lasting Soviet legacy. English is growing among younger generations but remains uncommon outside major hotels. Travelers are strongly advised to arrange a local guide.

Culture & Traditions

🪡

Carpet Weaving

Turkmen carpets are among the most celebrated in the world. The famous deep-red carpets with intricate geometric "gül" motifs are a national symbol. Ashgabat's Carpet Museum houses the world's largest handmade carpet. Weaving is traditionally a woman's art, passed mother to daughter across centuries.

🐎

The Akhal-Teke Horse

The Akhal-Teke is Turkmenistan's most revered national symbol — one of the world's oldest and rarest horse breeds, renowned for its metallic golden coat and extraordinary endurance. It features on the national emblem, and a national holiday celebrates this magnificent animal annually.

☽

Religion & Social Codes

The majority of Turkmens are Sunni Muslim, blended with pre-Islamic nomadic traditions. Society follows three codes: Adat (tribal law), Sherigat (Islamic law), and Edep (etiquette). Extended family is paramount, and hospitality to guests is sacred and non-negotiable.

Famous Delicacies

Plov

The beloved rice pilaf cooked with lamb, yellow carrots, onions, and fragrant cumin. The national dish served at every feast.

Shashlik

Marinated lamb or beef skewers grilled over charcoal. Found smoking at every roadside stand from dawn to dusk.

Somsa (Samsa)

Flaky baked pastries packed with minced lamb and onions — the quintessential bazaar snack.

Shurpa

A hearty mutton soup with potatoes and vegetables — rich, warming, and deeply comforting after a long desert journey.

Çay (Turkmen Tea)

Green tea — always green, never black — is the cornerstone of Turkmen hospitality. Served in small piala bowls; refusing a cup is considered deeply impolite. It binds every social interaction from business dealings to family gatherings.
